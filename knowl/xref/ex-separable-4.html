Example 1.6.4. Solve the IVP \(y' = xy^3(1 + x^2)^{-1/2}, \, y(\sqrt{3}) = \frac{1}{2}\).

We have obtained a one-parameter family of implicit solutions in ExampleÂ 1.6.3, namely,
\begin{equation*} -\frac{1}{2}y^2 = \sqrt{1 + x^2} + C. \end{equation*}
We shall continue from here.
  • We determine the constant \(C\) by using the initial condition:
    \begin{equation*} y(\sqrt{3}) = \frac{1}{2} \implies -\frac{1}{2} \cdot (1/2)^2 = \sqrt{1 + 3} + C \implies C = -4. \end{equation*}
  • The implicit solution we thus obtained is
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{1}{2y^2} = \sqrt{1 + x^2} - 4. \end{equation*}
    It is possible to solve an explicit solution. Take the negative reciprocal and simplify,
    \begin{equation*} y^2 = \frac{1}{2(4 - \sqrt{1 + x^2})}= \frac{1}{8 - 2\sqrt{1 + x^2}}. \end{equation*}
    Now take the square root. Since \(y(\sqrt{3}) = \frac{1}{2} \gt 0\text{,}\) we take the positive branch, thus
    \begin{equation*} y = \sqrt{\frac{1}{8 - 2\sqrt{1 + x^2}}}. \end{equation*}
  • Solution exists when
    \begin{align*} \amp 8 - 2\sqrt{1 + x^2} \gt 0 \implies 2\sqrt{1+x^2}\lt 8\implies 1+x^2 \lt 16 \\ \implies \amp x^2 \lt 15 \implies x \in (-\sqrt{15}, \sqrt{15}) \end{align*}
Conclusion: The solution is
\begin{equation*} y = \sqrt{\frac{1}{8 - 2\sqrt{1 + x^2}}}. \end{equation*}
The interval of definition is \((-\sqrt{15}, \sqrt{15})\text{.}\)
