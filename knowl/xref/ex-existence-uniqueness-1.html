Example 1.7.2. \(ty' + (t - 1)y = -e^{-t}, \, y(0) = 1\).

  • Standard form:
    \begin{equation*} y' + \dfrac{t - 1}{t}y = -\dfrac{e^{-t}}{t} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
  • Integrating factor:
    \begin{equation*} \mu(t) = e^{\int \dfrac{t - 1}{t}dt} = e^{\int (1 - \dfrac{1}{t})dt} = e^{t - \ln t} = \dfrac{e^t}{t} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
  • General solution:
    \begin{align*} y(t) = \amp \dfrac{\int e^{-t} \left(-\dfrac{e^{-t}}{t}\right)dt}{\dfrac{e^t}{t}} \\ = \amp te^{-t}\left(-\int t^{-2}dt\right)\\ = \amp te^{-t}\left(t^{-1} + C\right) = e^{-t} + Cte^{-t} \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
  • Plug in the initial value:
    \begin{equation*} y(0) = 1 \implies 1 + 0 = 1 \end{equation*}
    This means for every real number \(C\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = e^{-t} + Cte^{-t} \end{equation*}
    is a solution of the IVP. The IVP has infinitely many solutions.
