    Section 2.6 Second-order Linear Non-Homogeneous ODEs

    Subsection 2.6.1 Principle of Superposition and the Structure of the General Solution

    The general solution of a nonhomogeneous linear ODE
    \begin{equation*} y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = g(t) \end{equation*}
    is closely related to the solution of the homogeneous ODE
    \begin{equation*} y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = 0. \end{equation*}

    Theorem 2.6.1. Principle of Superposition.

    Let \(Y_1, Y_2\) be two solutions of
    \begin{equation*} y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = g(t). \end{equation*}
    Then \(Y_1 - Y_2\) is a solution of
    \begin{equation*} y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = 0. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    The assumption states that
    \begin{equation*} Y_1'' + p(t)Y_1' + q(t)Y_1 = g(t), \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} Y_2'' + p(t)Y_2' + q(t)Y_2 = g(t). \end{equation*}
    Subtract these two equations, by linearity of derivatives, we get
    \begin{equation*} (Y_1 - Y_2)'' + p(t)(Y_1 - Y_2)' + q(t)(Y_1 - Y_2) = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(Y_1 - Y_2\) is a solution of the \(y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = 0.\)

    Theorem 2.6.2. Structure of the general solution.

    The general solution of \(y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = g(t)\) is of the form
    \begin{equation*} y = y_c + Y = C_1y_1 + C_2y_2 + Y \end{equation*}
    where
    • \(y_c = C_1y_1 + C_2y_2\) is the general solution of the homogeneous ODE
      \begin{equation*} y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = 0. \end{equation*}
    • \(Y\) is a particular solution of ODE
      \begin{equation*} y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = g(t). \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    We first check that \(y = y_c + Y\) is a solution:
    \begin{align*} LHS \amp= (y_c + Y)'' + p(t)(y_c + Y)' + q(t)(y_c + Y)\\ \amp= y_c'' + p(t)y_c' + q(t)y_c + Y'' + p(t)Y' + q(t)Y\\ \amp= 0 + g(t) = RHS. \end{align*}
    It remains to check that any solution is of this form. Let \(y\) be any solution of the nonhomogeneous ODE. Then from the TheoremÂ 2.6.1, \(y - Y\) is a solution of the homogeneous ODE. Thus
    \begin{equation*} y - Y = C_1y_1 + C_2y_2 \end{equation*}
    for some numbers \(C_1, C_2.\) In other words,
    \begin{equation*} y = C_1y_1 + C_2y_2 + Y. \end{equation*}

    Definition 2.6.3.

    \(y_c = C_1y_1 + C_2y_2\) is called the complementary solution \(Y\) is called a particular solution.
    Solving a nonhomogeneous ODE reduces to two parts: Solve the homogeneous ODE to find the complementary solution, then find a particular solution.

    Subsection 2.6.2 Linear ODE with constant coefficients

    For a second order linear nonhomogeneous ODE with constant coefficients
    \begin{equation*} ay'' + by' + cy = g(t), \end{equation*}
    we learned how to find the complementary solution:
    We start by forming the auxiliary equation \(ar^2 + br + c = 0\) and solve the quadratic equation. Three cases:
    • Two distinct real roots \(r_1 \neq r_2,\) \(y_c = C_1e^{r_1t} + C_2e^{r_2t}.\)
    • Two distinct complex roots \(r_1 = \alpha + i\beta, r_2 = \alpha - i\beta,\) \(y_c = C_1e^{\alpha t} \cos \beta t + C_2e^{\alpha t} \sin \beta t.\)
    • Two repeated real roots \(r_1 = r_2 = r,\) \(y_c = C_1e^{rt} + C_2te^{rt}.\)
    For a second order linear nonhomogeneous ODE with constant coefficients
    \begin{equation*} ay'' + by' + cy = g(t), \end{equation*}
    There are two approaches of finding a particular solution.
    1. Method of Undetermined Coefficients. The method is convenient and easy to generalize to higher-order linear ODEs, but only works when all the coefficients are constant and \(g(t)\) ranges in special classes of functions. Nevertheless, these special cases are good enough for lots of engineering practices.
    2. Variation of Parameter (not required). Knowing the complementary solution \(y_c = C_1y_1 + C_2y_2,\) we may set \(Y = u_1y_1 + u_2y_2\) and solve that
      \begin{equation*} Y = y_1 \cdot \int \dfrac{-y_2 \cdot g}{W(y_1, y_2)} dt + y_2 \cdot \int \dfrac{y_1 \cdot g}{W(y_1, y_2)} dt \end{equation*}
      Therefore, knowing one part of the complementary solution, we may apply the reduction of order in SubsectionÂ 2.5.2 to find the full complementary solution, then use this formula to find the particular solution.
      While this integration formula is powerful and can be generalized to higher-order linear ODEs, the formula in higher-order case is much more complicated and not so convenient to use.
