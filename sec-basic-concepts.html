    Section 1.1 Basic Concepts

    Definition 1.1.1.

    A differential equation is an equation concerning derivatives. An ordinary differential equation (abbrev. ODE) is an equation involving only ordinary derivatives of a single-variable function.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Here is the typical form of a first-order ODE:
    \begin{equation*} y'(t) = f(t,y(t)). \end{equation*}
    or, with the abbreviation of \(y(t)\) and \(y'(t)\) by \(y\) and \(y'\text{,}\) respectively,
    \begin{equation*} y' = f(t,y) \end{equation*}
    where
    ðŸ”—
    • \(t\) is the independent variable.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(y = y(t)\) is a one-variable function depending on \(t\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(y' = \frac{dy}{dt}\) is the derivative of \(y\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(f(t,y)\) is a known function of two variables.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    This ODE expresses the derivative \(y'(t)\) in terms of \(t\) and \(y(t)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 1.1.2.

    A function \(y(t)\) defined on an open interval \(I\) is called a solution of the ODE \(y' = f(t,y)\) on \(I\) if
    ðŸ”—
    • \(y(t)\) is differentiable on \(I\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • For all \(t\) in \(I\text{,}\) the equation \(y' = f(t,y)\) holds.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    While we havenâ€™t learned how to solve an ODE, it is easy to check if a function is a solution of an ODE. Simply compute its derivatives, then plug in the ODE, and see if equality holds.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.3. A solution of an ODE.

    \(y(t) = e^t\) is a solution of the ODE \(y' - y = 0\text{,}\) because \(y'(t) = e^t\text{,}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \(\implies LHS = y' - y = e^t - e^t = 0 = RHS\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The solution is defined on everywhere on \((-\infty, \infty)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.4. A non-solution of an ODE.

    \(y(t) = \cos 2t\) is not a solution of the ODE \(y' - y = 0\text{,}\) because \(y'(t) = -2\sin 2t\text{,}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \(\implies LHS = y' - y = -2\sin 2t - \cos 2t \neq 0 = RHS\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The solution is defined on everywhere on \((-\infty, \infty)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 1.1.5.

    We shall apply the method of checking solutions extensively in this course, especially in the study of higher-order linear ODEs.

    ðŸ”—

    Definition 1.1.6.

    Most generally, a first-order ODE is of the form
    \begin{equation*} F(t,y,y') = 0. \end{equation*}
    i.e., a relation among the variable \(t, y,\) and \(y'\text{.}\) We call this the general form of a first-order ODE.
    ðŸ”—
    The order of a differential equation is the order of the highest derivative appearing in the equation. Similarly, the general form of an \(n\)-th order ODE is
    \begin{equation*} F(t,y,y',...,y^{(n)}) = 0 \end{equation*}
    i.e., a relation among the variable \(t, y\) and the \(n\) derivatives \(y', y'', \cdots, y^{(n)}\) of \(y\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.7. Examples of ODEs in typical and general forms.

    • \(y' = t^2 + y\) (First-order ODE in its typical form).
      ðŸ”—
    • \((y')^2 + \sin y = y + t^2\) (First-order ODE).
      ðŸ”—
    • \(y''' + 3y'' + 3y' + y = e^{2t}\) (Third-order ODE).

    Definition 1.1.8.

    A partial differential equation (abbrev. PDE) is an equation concerning partial derivatives.

    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} = 0. \end{equation*}
