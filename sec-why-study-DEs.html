    Section 1.2 Why study differential equations?

    The answer is simple: Differential equation is the foundation of science and technology. In this section, we shall discuss two examples.

    Subsection 1.2.1 Newtonâ€™s law of cooling

    Consider an object of temperature \(T\) placed in a room of ambient temperature \(T_a\text{.}\) Newtonâ€™s law of cooling states that the rate of change of \(T\) is proportional to the difference of the temperatures between the object and the ambient room. Mathematically, this means
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a), \end{equation*}
    where \(k\) is some constant, \(T_a\) is the ambient temperature.
    If the ambient temperature \(T_a\) is constant, then we may solve the ODE by separating the variables:
    \begin{align*} \amp \dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a) \\ \implies \amp \dfrac{dT}{T - T_a} = -kdt \\ \implies \amp \int \dfrac{dT}{T - T_a} = \int -kdt \\ \implies \amp \ln|T - T_a| = -kt + C \end{align*}
    We exponentiate both sides to obtain
    \begin{equation} T - T_a = Ce^{-kt}\tag{1.2.1} \end{equation}
    Then moving \(T_a\) to the right-hand side, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} T = T_a + Ce^{-kt} \end{equation*}

    Remark 1.2.1.

    • The constant \(C\) is arbitrary. Without any further information there is no way to decide \(C\text{.}\)
    • In other words, \(T(t) = T_a + C e^{-kt}\) is a solution of the ODE for ANY \(C\). Such an expression is called a one-parameter family of solutions. Generally, when solving a first-order ODE \(F(t, y, y') = 0\text{,}\) we seek a one-parameter family of solutions. If all solutions of the ODE are contained in the family, then we call it the general solution.

    Remark 1.2.2.

    • When we exponentiated to obtain (1.2.1), we ignored the absolute value after exponentiation. Letâ€™s go through the details carefully. Rigorously, when we exponentiate both sides of the equation, we get
      \begin{equation*} |T - T_a| = e^{-kt + C} = e^C e^{-kt} \end{equation*}
      Notice that \(e^C\) is always a positive constant. Getting rid of the absolute value, we obtain
      \begin{equation*} T - T_a = \pm e^C e^{-kt} \end{equation*}
      What we did to obtain (1.2.1) is to incorporate the constant \(\pm e^C\) into the constant \(C\text{.}\) So the constant \(C\) can be either positive or negative.
    • A more acute reader might ask can \(C\) be zero? The answer is yes. If \(C = 0\text{,}\) then \(T = T_a\text{,}\) which is a constant function. It is easily verified that \(T = T_a\) is also a solution of the ODE.

    Subsection 1.2.2 Water pollution model

    A lake has water volume \(V\) \(\text{m}^3\text{.}\) Assume \(V\) is constant. A factory emits \(R\) kilograms of mercury into the lake every day. Suppose the mercury diffuses to the lake instantly and water refreshes every day by \(W\)\(\text{m}^3\text{.}\) How much time does it take for the water to be unpotable?
    Let \(P(t)\) be the mass of mercury in the lake at time \(t\text{.}\) Let \(\Delta t\) be a small period of time. Within the time period \(\Delta t\text{:}\)
    • Mercury in = \(R \Delta t\text{.}\)
    • Polluted water out = \(W \Delta t\text{.}\)
    • Density of mercury = \(\dfrac{P(t)}{V}\text{.}\)
    • Mercury out = \(\dfrac{P(t)}{V} W \Delta t\text{.}\)
    Thus
    \begin{align*} \amp \Delta P(t) = R \Delta t - \dfrac{P(t)}{V} W \\ \implies \amp \dfrac{\Delta P(t)}{\Delta t} = R - \dfrac{P(t)}{V} W \end{align*}
    Let \(\Delta t \to 0\text{,}\) we obtain a differential equation for \(P(t)\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dP(t)}{dt} = R - \dfrac{P(t)}{V} W \end{equation*}
    Although not explicitly stated in the problem, we actually have
    • An initial condition \(P(0) = 0\text{.}\)
    • We may also search on internet for the EPA standard, which states that water is not potable if density of mercury reaches the level \(0.002\, \mathrm{mg/L}\text{.}\)
    Therefore, we are looking for a time \(T\) when
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{P(T)}{V} \geq 0.002\, \mathrm{mg/L} = 2 \times 10^{-6}\, \mathrm{kg/m^3} \end{equation*}
    This differential equation can be solved by separating variables:
    \begin{align*} \amp \dfrac{dP}{dt} = R - \dfrac{P}{V}W \\ \implies \amp \dfrac{dP}{R - \dfrac{P}{V}W} = dt \\ \implies \amp \dfrac{V dP}{W P - V R} = -dt \\ \implies \amp \int \dfrac{V dP}{-W P + V R} = \int dt \\ \implies \amp W P - V R = C e^{-\frac{W}{V} t} \\ \implies \amp P(t) = \dfrac{V}{W} R + C e^{-\dfrac{W}{V}t}. \end{align*}
    This is a one-parameter family of solutions of the ODE. We may determine the constant \(C\) with the initial condition:
    \begin{equation*} P(0) = 0 \implies \dfrac{V}{W} R + C = 0 \implies C = -\dfrac{V}{W} R \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} P(t) = \dfrac{V}{W} R - \dfrac{V}{W} R e^{-\frac{W}{V} t}, t \geq 0. \end{equation*}
    We obtained a definite function together with an interval.
    In case \(V = 10^6\, \mathrm{m}^3\text{,}\) \(W = 2 \times 10^4\, \mathrm{m}^3/\mathrm{d}\text{,}\) \(R = 0.05\, \mathrm{kg}/\mathrm{d}\text{,}\) we may solve that
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{P(T)}{V} \geq 0.002\, \mathrm{mg/L} = 2 \times 10^{-6}\, \mathrm{kg/m^3} \end{equation*}
    when \(T \geq -\dfrac{1}{0.02} \ln \dfrac{1}{5} \approx 80.5\, \mathrm{day}\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.2.3.

    We have solved the initial value problem
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dP(t)}{dt} = R - \dfrac{P(t)}{V} W, \quad P(0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    which is an ODE together with an initial condition. The solution is a function defined on an interval satisfying both the ODE and the initial condition.
    We end this section with the following definition.

    Definition 1.2.4.

    • A first-order ODE normally have infinitely many solutions. Many of them can be represented as one-parameter family of solutions. If the one-parameter family contains all solutions, we call it the general solution.
    • A first order initial value problem is of the form
      \begin{equation*} y' = f(t, y), \quad y(t_0) = y_0 \end{equation*}
      In most science and engineering applications, an IVP should have a unique solution on an interval, called interval of existence and uniqueness, usually abbreviated as interval of existence.
    • Generally, an \(n\)-th order ODE also have infinitely many solutions. Many of them can be represented as an \(n\)-parameter family of solutions. If the \(n\)-parameter family contains all solutions, we call it the general solution.
    • Generally, an initial value problem (which shall be abbreviated as IVP) consists of a (set of) differential equation(s) and a (set of) initial values. In case the IVP contains one ODE of order \(n\text{,}\) we need \(n\) initial values to obtain a unique solution.
