    Section 1.2 Why study differential equations?

    The answer is simple: Differential equation is the foundation of science and technology.

    Subsection 1.2.1 Newton’s law of cooling

    Consider an object of temperature \(T\) placed in a room of ambient temperature \(T_a\text{.}\)
    Newton’s law of cooling: The rate of change of \(T\) is proportional to the difference of the temperatures between the object and the ambient room.
    Mathematically, this means
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a), \end{equation*}
    where \(k\) is some constant, \(T_a\) is the ambient temperature.
    If the ambient temperature \(T_a\) is constant, then we may solve it by separating the variables:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a) \implies \frac{dT}{T - T_a} = -kdt \implies \int \frac{dT}{T - T_a} = \int -kdt \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \implies \ln|T - T_a| = -kt + C \implies T - T_a = Ce^{-kt} \implies T = T_a + Ce^{-kt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note: The constant \(C\) is arbitrary. Without any further information there is no way to decide \(C\text{.}\) In other words, \(T(t) = T_a + C e^{-kt}\) is a solution of the ODE for ANY \(C\). Such an expression is called a one-parameter family of solutions. Generally, when solving a first-order ODE \(F(t, y, y') = 0\text{,}\) we seek a one-parameter family of solutions. If all solutions of the ODE are contained in the family, then we call it the general solution.

    Subsection 1.2.2 Water pollution model

    A lake has water volume \(V\) m3. Assume \(V\) is constant. A factory emits \(R\) kilograms of mercury into the lake every day. Suppose the mercury diffuses to the lake instantly and water refreshes every day by \(W\) m3. How much time does it take for the water to be unpotable?
    Let \(P(t)\) be the mass of mercury in the lake at time \(t\text{.}\) Let \(\Delta t\) be a small period of time. Within the time period \(\Delta t\text{:}\)
    • Mercury in = \(R \Delta t\text{.}\)
    • Polluted water out = \(W \Delta t\text{.}\)
    • Density of mercury = \(\frac{P(t)}{V}\text{.}\)
    • Mercury out = \(\frac{P(t)}{V} W \Delta t\text{.}\)
    Thus
    \begin{equation*} \Delta P(t) = R \Delta t - \frac{P(t)}{V} W \implies \frac{\Delta P(t)}{\Delta t} = R - \frac{P(t)}{V} W \end{equation*}
    Let \(\Delta t \to 0\text{,}\) we obtain a differential equation for \(P(t)\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP(t)}{dt} = R - \frac{P(t)}{V} W \end{equation*}
    Although not explicitly stated in the problem, we actually have
    • An initial condition \(P(0) = 0\text{.}\)
    • We may also search on internet for the EPA standard, which states that water is not potable if density of mercury reaches the level \(0.002\, \mathrm{mg/L}\text{.}\)
    Therefore, we are looking for a time \(T\) when
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(T)}{V} \geq 0.002\, \mathrm{mg/L} = 2 \times 10^{-6}\, \mathrm{kg/m^3} \end{equation*}
    This differential equation can be solved by separating variables:
    \begin{align*} \amp \frac{dP}{dt} = R - \frac{P}{V}W \implies \frac{dP}{R - \frac{P}{V}W} = dt \implies \frac{V dP}{W P - V R} = -dt \\ \implies \amp \int \frac{V dP}{-W P + V R} = \int dt \implies \frac{V}{W} \cdot \ln|W P - V R| = t + C \\ \implies \amp W P - V R = C e^{-\frac{W}{V} t} \implies P(t) = \frac{V}{W} R + C e^{-\frac{W}{V}t}. \end{align*}
    This is a one-parameter family of solutions of the ODE. We may determine the constant \(C\) with the initial condition:
    \begin{equation*} P(0) = 0 \implies \frac{V}{W} R + C = 0 \implies C = -\frac{V}{W} R \implies P(t) = \frac{V}{W} R - \frac{V}{W} R e^{-\frac{W}{V} t} \end{equation*}
    So we obtained a definite function as the solution.
    In case \(V = 10^6\, \mathrm{m}^3\text{,}\) \(W = 2 \times 10^4\, \mathrm{m}^3/\mathrm{d}\text{,}\) \(R = 0.05\, \mathrm{kg}/\mathrm{d}\text{,}\) we see that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(T)}{V} \geq 0.002\, \mathrm{mg/L} = 2 \times 10^{-6}\, \mathrm{kg/m^3} \end{equation*}
    when \(T \geq -\frac{1}{0.02} \ln \frac{1}{5} \approx 80.5\, \mathrm{day}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.2.3 Initial value problem

    We have solved the initial value problem
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP(t)}{dt} = R - \frac{P(t)}{V} W, \quad P(0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    Generally, an initial value problem (which shall be abbreviated as IVP) consists of a (set of) differential equation(s) and a (set of) initial values.
    A first order IVP is of the form
    \begin{equation*} y' = f(t, y), \quad y(t_0) = y_0 \end{equation*}
    In most science and engineering applications, an IVP should have a unique solution on an interval, called interval of existence.
    Summary:
    A first-order ODE \(\implies\) One-parameter family of solutions.
    A first-order IVP \(\implies\) A particular solution + interval of existence.
